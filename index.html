Hydrology & Hydraulics Overview

1. Introduction to Hydrology

Hydrology is the scientific study of water's properties, distribution, and effects on the earth's surface, soils, and atmosphere. It encompasses the study of the hydrological cycle, water resources, and environmental watershed sustainability.

2. The Hydrological Cycle

This cycle describes the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. The process is significant due to its role in the distribution of water resources.

  • Precipitation: Moisture released from clouds.
  • Interception: Water caught by plant leaves.
  • Infiltration: Water soaks into the soil.
  • Surface Runoff: Water flows over the land.
  • Evapotranspiration: Water evaporates from surfaces and transpires from plants.

3. Water Movement

Water moves in various ways, significantly affecting hydrological and environmental outcomes:

  • Throughfall and Stemflow: Water pathways in vegetation areas.
  • Groundwater Flow: Movement through soil layers and aquifers.

4. Rainfall Analysis

Understanding rainfall patterns is crucial for managing water resources and mitigating flood risks.

  • Intensity, Duration, and Frequency: Key metrics that determine the impact of rainfall events.
  • Measurement Tools: Rain Gauges collect and measure rainfall.

5. Runoff Analysis

Runoff is part of the water cycle, involving the flow of water from land to water bodies, and is influenced by several factors:

  • Soil type, land topography, and vegetation are crucial in determining runoff volume and speed.
  • Methodologies like the Rational Method are used to estimate peak discharge from small catchment areas.

6. Probability and Recurrence Intervals

These concepts are essential in hydrological predictions:

  • Recurrence Interval: The average time between events (like heavy rainfalls).
  • Probability of Occurrence: Calculated to assess the risk of hydrological events.

7. Stormwater Management

Effective stormwater management is crucial for reducing runoff and improving water quality:

  • Detention and Retention Basins: Structures that manage runoff and mitigate its effects.

8. Drainage Systems Design

Drainage systems are designed to manage water efficiently, ensuring safety and functionality:

  • Hydraulic Design: Considers factors like pipe flow capacity and network layout.

9. Hydrographs

A hydrograph plots the rate of flow versus time for a given point on a stream or river, particularly useful for predicting future water scenarios.

10. Environmental and Urban Impacts

Development significantly affects hydrological systems:

  • Urbanization increases runoff and reduces infiltration, exacerbating flood risks.
  • Mitigation Techniques: Green infrastructures like permeable pavements and rain gardens.
